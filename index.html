For the data geeks

The dominance region

Topographic isolation reduces a summit to a single number: the distance to its nearest higher neighbour. The dominance region generalises that number into a two dimensional region, the ground a summit owns before higher terrain takes over, bounded by the higher peaks that surround it.

P is the summit at elevation h_P. H(P) is the set of catalogued peaks strictly higher than P and beyond a 2 km self exclusion. For each higher peak q at angular distance d_q and bearing alpha_q, and for each of 1,440 compass bearings theta (every 0.25 degrees), rho selects the higher peak that most tightly bounds that bearing. The winners V are the summit's higher neighbours and the polygon's vertices; the boundary is swept through them, and its area follows the spherical integral above. The narrowest radius equals the topographic isolation.

How it differs from Voronoi and Thiessen

A Voronoi or Thiessen boundary falls halfway between points. The dominance region boundary runs through the higher peaks themselves, because they are its vertices. It is ego centric (one summit's region, not a global tessellation), its generators are attribute filtered (only higher peaks qualify), and its narrowest radius is the isolation distance. It is built on established parts, spherical Voronoi and Delaunay neighbours and topographic isolation. Using this specific region as an isolation footprint is the new part.

Copy the definition

LaTeX source, for anyone who wants to reproduce or extend it:

H(P) = \{\, q : h_q > h_P,\ \delta(P,q) > \varepsilon \,\}
\rho(\theta,q) = \operatorname{atan2}\!\left(\tan(d_q/2),\ \cos(\theta-\alpha_q)\right)
j(\theta) = \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{q \in H}\ \rho(\theta,q)
V = \{\, q \in H : \exists\, \theta,\ j(\theta)=q \,\}
E(P) = \text{spherical polygon through } V \text{ ordered by } \alpha
A(E) = R^2 \int_{0}^{2\pi}\bigl(1-\cos r(\theta)\bigr)\,d\theta
\min_{\theta} r(\theta) = \operatorname{isolation}(P)

Extend it

The same construction applies to all catalogued peaks rather than these 40, to submarine seamounts, to base to peak height instead of sea level elevation, and to other planetary bodies.

Isolation and higher neighbours

Isolation is the great circle distance to the nearest higher catalogued peak, which is the shortest spoke. Each of the 1,440 bearings is won by the higher peak that most tightly bounds it; the winners are the summit's higher neighbours and the polygon's vertices.

Reading the map

Spokes run from the summit (the hub) to each higher neighbour, coloured by distance; the nearest is the isolation. The shaded region is the dominance region. Web mode shows every summit's spokes at once.

Why Aconcagua looks like the whole world with a hole

Aconcagua's higher neighbours are all clustered in one direction, the Himalaya and Karakoram, thousands of km away across Asia. With no higher ground in any other direction, its dominance region wraps almost the entire planet and leaves only a small excluded cap near its antipode. That is why its dominance region renders as the world minus a hole, and why it tops the dominance area ranking.

Data and caveats

Higher peaks come from the GeoNames allCountries gazetteer (feature codes PK, PKS, MT, VLC, HLL), with a stale elevation guard (rejecting entries whose listed elevation exceeds the DEM by more than 1,000 m) and two corrected coordinates (Mascarin Peak to Marion Island; Joe's Hill to Kiritimati). Coverage is thinnest near sea level and over Antarctica, so a few summits (for example Mawson Peak) reflect the nearest higher catalogued peak rather than a terrain traced ridge, and can differ from the Wikipedia figure.
